MMM in Practice: Optimizing $50M in QSR Media Spend

December 2025‚Ä¢15 min read
Marketing Mix Modeling sounds theoretical until you're responsible for allocating real budgets. Here's how I'd approach optimizing a $50M media budget across TV, social, search, and digital channels‚Äîwith real QSR constraints, lag effects, and incrementality challenges.

The Problem: Attribution Is Broken in QSR

A customer sees a TV ad during the Super Bowl, searches for "subs near me" two days later, clicks a paid search ad, downloads the app, then orders in-store the following week. Which channel gets credit?

Last-click attribution says: paid search. Multi-touch says: split it. MMM says: all of them contributed, but TV had the biggest incremental impact. Let's measure that.

Why Traditional Attribution Fails for Restaurants

1. Offline conversion majority: 60%+ of QSR orders still happen in-store or drive-thru, not digitally. Can't cookie-track a drive-thru order.

2. Long consideration windows: TV ad ‚Üí app download might be 2 weeks. Search attribution windows are 30 days max.

3. Geographic fragmentation: Media is national or DMA-level. Orders are location-specific. Connecting the dots requires store-level data.

4. Baseline vs incrementality: If you spend $10M on TV and sales go up 5%, how much was organic growth vs TV-driven? Attribution can't tell you. MMM can.

The MMM Approach: Regression on Steroids

At its core, MMM is multivariate regression: Y (sales) = f(TV spend, digital spend, search spend, seasonality, promotions, competitor activity, weather, etc.)

The model isolates each variable's contribution to sales while controlling for everything else. But the devil is in the details.

Data Requirements

2-3 years of weekly data: Need enough observations to detect patterns. Monthly data works but weekly is better.

Spend by channel: TV, radio, social (paid), search (paid), display, OOH, email, app push, etc.

Impressions/GRPs: Spend alone isn't enough. $1M in TV during the Super Bowl ‚â† $1M in late-night cable.

Sales data: Ideally broken out by geography (DMA or store-level) and channel (in-store, app, web, kiosk).

External factors: Seasonality (holidays, weather), competitor activity (promotions, new locations), macroeconomic indicators (unemployment, gas prices).

The Hard Parts: Lag Effects

TV doesn't drive sales instantly. There's a lag‚Äîusually 1-4 weeks depending on the message. "New menu item" ads have shorter lags (1 week). Brand ads have longer lags (4-8 weeks).

The model needs to test different lag structures (called "adstock" in MMM). You're asking: does this week's TV spend impact this week's sales? Next week? Two weeks from now? All three?

We use Adstock transformations‚Äîexponential decay functions that model how advertising effectiveness fades over time. A strong ad might have 50% retention after 1 week, 25% after 2 weeks, 12% after 3 weeks.

Isolating Incrementality

The key question MMM answers: if we cut TV spend by $5M, what happens to sales?

Not what happens to "attributed" sales. What happens to TOTAL sales, including the organic baseline that would've occurred anyway.

This requires baseline modeling. We separate sales into:

  • Base sales: What you'd get with zero marketing (brand equity, word of mouth, location traffic)
  • Incremental sales: Lift from each marketing channel above the baseline

If TV drives $8M incremental sales on $5M spend, that's a 1.6x ROI. If social drives $3M on $5M spend, that's 0.6x. Now you know where to reallocate.

The $50M Budget Optimization

Let's say current allocation:

  • TV: $20M (40%)
  • Digital (social + display): $15M (30%)
  • Search: $10M (20%)
  • Other (radio, OOH, email): $5M (10%)

MMM reveals:

  • TV ROI: 1.4x (diminishing returns kicking in at current spend level)
  • Digital ROI: 0.8x (oversaturated, too much frequency waste)
  • Search ROI: 2.1x (undersaturated, room to grow)
  • Other: 1.1x (radio works, OOH doesn't, email is gold)

The Optimization Move

Shift $5M from Digital to Search (increase search to $15M). Cut $3M from TV (down to $17M, still above saturation floor). Reallocate $2M from OOH to email. New predicted ROI: 1.3x overall vs 1.1x current.

Expected incremental revenue: $10M annually from reallocation alone, no additional spend.

QSR-Specific Challenges

1. Franchise vs Corporate: Corporate owns national media (TV, digital). Franchisees own local (radio, OOH). Aligning incentives is hard. MMM helps by showing which channels drive store traffic.

2. Menu complexity: Limited time offers (LTOs) complicate the model. You need to separate "sales lift from new sandwich" vs "sales lift from the ad promoting it."

3. Cannibalization: Digital orders might cannibalize in-store. MMM should model total sales, not just digital, to avoid optimizing a zero-sum game.

4. Competitor activity: If Burger King runs a massive promotion, your sales drop regardless of your media. Model needs competitor spend/promo data to isolate your true impact.

Advanced Techniques

Bayesian MMM: Traditional regression assumes fixed coefficients. Bayesian MMM models uncertainty‚Äîuseful when you have limited data or want to incorporate prior beliefs (e.g., "we know TV ROI is between 1.2-1.8x").

Geo-level modeling: Instead of national MMM, run separate models per DMA or cluster of DMAs. Captures regional differences (TV works better in Midwest, digital in coastal cities).

Dynamic coefficients: Allow channel effectiveness to vary over time. Useful when creative changes or market saturation shifts.

Implementation Roadmap

Month 1-2: Data collection and cleaning. This is 50% of the work. Aligning spend data with sales data across systems is painful.

Month 3: Model build. Test different lag structures, adstock curves, baseline assumptions.

Month 4: Validation. Holdout testing (remove last 3 months of data, see if model predicts it accurately).

Month 5: Scenario planning. Run "what if" analyses: what if we cut TV 20%? What if we double search?

Month 6+: Ongoing monitoring. Rerun model quarterly as new data comes in. Media effectiveness changes‚Äîmodels need updates.

üí° The Reality Check

MMM isn't a crystal ball. It's a tool. Models are wrong (all models are), but useful. The goal isn't perfect attribution‚Äîit's better decision-making. If MMM says "search is underinvested" and you test it and ROI improves, the model was useful even if it wasn't 100% accurate.

Pair MMM with incrementality tests (geo holdouts, test/control markets) to validate findings. MMM guides strategy. Tests prove it.

Tools & Vendors

  • Analytic Partners, Nielsen, Neustar: Enterprise MMM vendors. Expensive but robust.
  • Recast, Streamline: Modern SaaS MMM tools. Easier to use, lower cost.
  • Build your own: Python (statsmodels, PyMC for Bayesian), R (prophet, MASS). Requires data science chops.

The Bottom Line

If you're spending $50M on media, you can't afford NOT to do MMM. Even a 5% improvement in ROI is $2.5M annually. The cost of MMM? $200-500K for vendor + internal time. That's a 5-10x return on the analysis itself.

Attribution tells you what happened. MMM tells you what to do next. For budget allocation, that's the difference that matters.
