The Starting Point (2022)

When I joined as Senior Digital Experience Strategist, digital represented just 6% of total revenue. Industry leaders were at 15-20%. We had infrastructure—mobile app, web ordering, kiosk—but not optimization. Conversion rates were mediocre. AOV was stagnant. Attribution was broken.

The challenge wasn't technical debt. It was strategic fragmentation. Different teams owned different pieces without a unified North Star metric.

Digital Share Growth Trajectory (2022-2025)

The 18-Month Roadmap

Phase 1: Fix the Foundation (Months 1-6)
Fixed critical bugs causing checkout drops. Standardized UTM frameworks. Resolved latency issues recovering $1M+ monthly. Implemented proper Adobe Analytics tagging. No glamorous work, but essential.

Phase 2: Optimize the Core (Months 7-12)
Launched systematic A/B testing using Adobe Target. Tested everything: store locator defaults, combo prompts, offer placements, checkout flows. Ran 50+ concurrent tests without breaking things.

Phase 3: Personalize the Experience (Months 13-18)
Implemented recommendation strategies. Sequenced 1:1 offers vs national promotions. Personalized product discovery. Reduced privacy opt-outs by 300bps. Digital share accelerated from 9% to 12%.

Key Initiatives That Moved the Needle

1. Store Locator Optimization (+200bps)

Simple hypothesis: most guests reorder from the same location. We defaulted the store locator to their most recent order location. Result: +200 basis points conversion lift.

2. Offer Sequencing Strategy

1:1 offers outperformed generic promotions by 280 bps for Gen Z, 190 bps for Millennials. But generic offers worked better for first-timers needing social proof. Solution: dynamic offer logic by cohort.

3. AOV Growth Through Upsell Placement

Cart-based prompts timed to trigger when cart value hit $8-$10 lifted AOV from $13 to $15, adding $2 per transaction across millions of orders annually.

What Didn't Work

  • Over-personalization: Hyper-personalized menu recommendations felt creepy, not helpful. Conversion dropped 40 bps.
  • Push notifications: Aggressive frequency (3x/week) led to 18% app uninstalls. Scaled back to 1x/week max.
  • Gamification: "Streak" mechanics didn't sustain. QSR isn't gaming—people want food, not badges.

The Operating Model

We built an agile pod model: 2 FTE strategists owning roadmap, 5 embedded Martech consultants executing, weekly sprint planning via Azure DevOps, monthly business reviews with senior leadership.

Lessons for Other QSR Brands

1. Fix before you optimize: 6 months on infrastructure made everything else possible.

2. Pick one North Star: Digital share unified teams.

3. Test everything: Even "obvious" improvements need validation.

4. Personalization is a spectrum: Start simple, add complexity only when simpler approaches plateau.

5. Build for scale: Pods, sprints, SLAs—these aren't bureaucracy, they're enablers.
