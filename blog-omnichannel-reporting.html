Are We Spending Our Marketing Budget Efficiently? Why Nobody Can Answer (And How to Fix It)

February 22, 2026•12 min read
When I started asking questions about our Marketing Mix Modeling and media attribution—who owns what, how decisions get made, where the data lives—I noticed something: we had multiple vendors providing pieces of the puzzle, but no single source of truth. Our Chief Data Officer noticed it too. And he wasn't happy about the vendor dependency. Here's what a solution could look like.

Note: Specific figures and metrics in this article have been adjusted to protect confidential business information while preserving the framework and strategic approach.

The Vendor Dependency Problem

One vendor handles Marketing Mix Modeling. The media agency provides attribution data from different channels. Each report claims conversions. The data doesn't always align. There's no unified view that our in-house analytics team controls.

Then our new head of CRM and Paid Media asks a straightforward question: "Are we spending our marketing budget efficiently?"

Nobody has a clear answer. Not because we lack data—we have too much, from too many sources, with too many conflicting stories.

❌ The Current State

→ Multiple vendors, each with their own data
→ Conflicting attribution claims
→ Can't validate one vendor against another
→ Leadership questions require vendor alignment
→ No single source of truth we control

✓ What We Need

→ Unified data we own
→ One reporting layer for all channels
→ Real-time executive dashboard
→ Clear metrics: CAC, LTV, ROAS
→ In-house analytics control

This isn't about replacing vendors—their expertise is valuable. It's about bringing the data together in a way we control, so we can actually answer leadership's questions.

The 3 Metrics That Matter

To answer "Are we spending efficiently?", you need three core metrics:

1. Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

What it measures: How much you spend to acquire one customer

CAC = Total Marketing & Sales Costs / New Customers Acquired

Why leadership cares: CAC directly impacts profitability. If it costs more to acquire a customer than they're worth, you're losing money.

The omnichannel challenge: Marketing costs are spread across paid media, organic, CRM, technology, and personnel. Most companies only track paid media costs, systematically under-reporting true CAC.

2. Lifetime Value (LTV)

What it measures: How much profit a customer generates over their entire relationship with you

LTV = (Average Order Value × Purchase Frequency × Gross Margin) × Average Customer Lifespan

Why leadership cares: LTV determines how much you can afford to spend on acquisition. Higher LTV means you can outspend competitors on customer acquisition.

The omnichannel challenge: Customers interact across web, app, kiosk, and in-store. If you can't connect transactions across channels, you're under-counting LTV and making bad acquisition decisions.

3. Return on Ad Spend (ROAS)

What it measures: Revenue generated per dollar of advertising spend

ROAS = Revenue Attributable to Marketing / Marketing Spend

Why leadership cares: ROAS shows marketing efficiency. A 3:1 ROAS means every dollar spent generates $3 in revenue.

The omnichannel challenge: Attribution. Did the TV ad, social post, email, or paid search drive the sale? When multiple vendors claim the same conversion, ROAS gets inflated.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Mistake #1: Incomplete Cost Attribution

If you only count paid media spend but not martech licenses and team salaries, your CAC is wrong. Companies often under-report CAC by 40% this way.

Mistake #2: Letting Vendors Claim the Same Conversion

When your MMM vendor, media agency, and platform reports all claim credit for the same sale, your ROAS looks inflated. You need one attribution model, not three conflicting ones.

Mistake #3: Ignoring Offline Channels

In QSR, 60%+ of orders are still offline. If your omnichannel reporting only tracks digital, you're missing more than half the picture.

The Bottom Line

Vendors provide valuable expertise. But when they each own pieces of your reporting, nobody can answer simple questions about efficiency.

The solution: build unified omnichannel reporting that your in-house analytics team controls. Focus on three metrics leadership cares about: CAC, LTV, and ROAS. Make it real-time. Make it automated. Give your CDO the ability to answer business questions without reconciling three vendor reports.

"Are we spending our marketing budget efficiently?" shouldn't take three weeks and a vendor meeting to answer.

That's what omnichannel reporting—owned by you—makes possible.
