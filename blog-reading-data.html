The Fantasy Dominance

One pattern emerges immediately: I'm a fantasy reader through and through. Brandon Sanderson's Mistborn series, Pierce Brown's Red Rising saga, Patrick Rothfuss's Name of the Wind‚Äîthese aren't just books I've read, they're worlds I've inhabited. My favorites shelf tells the story: epic fantasy series dominate, with occasional detours into literary fiction and sci-fi.

Genre Distribution Across 204 Read Books

What's interesting from an analytics perspective is the "binge pattern." When I discover an author I love, I don't just read one book‚ÄîI consume their entire bibliography. Brandon Sanderson's Cosmere universe, Joe Abercrombie's First Law books, the complete Red Rising series. This mirrors consumer behavior I see in QSR digital: once someone finds their preferred ordering pattern, they repeat it obsessively.

Reading Velocity & Life Patterns

My reading rate isn't consistent‚Äîit tracks directly with stress levels and major life events. High-activity work periods correlate with comfort rereads (hello again, Mistborn). Vacation periods show experimentation with new genres. The pandemic years? A massive surge in consumption as books became the primary escape.

The Sanderson Effect

Let's talk about author loyalty. Brandon Sanderson appears multiple times in my favorites, and his books have an average page count of 500+. That's not casual reading‚Äîthat's commitment. But here's what the data shows: high page counts don't correlate with low ratings for me. In fact, my 5-star reviews skew toward longer books. I'm not a quick-read person; I'm an immersion person.

Page Count vs. My Rating

This has implications for how I think about user experience design. Conventional wisdom says "shorter is better"‚Äîreduce friction, minimize steps, get to conversion fast. But my reading data suggests something more nuanced: the right people will engage deeply with rich, complex experiences if the quality is there. It's not about shortening the journey; it's about making the journey worth taking.

The Outliers

Not everything is fantasy. "Tomorrow, and Tomorrow, and Tomorrow" by Gabrielle Zevin sits prominently in my favorites‚Äîa literary fiction novel about friendship, creativity, and video games. "The Unbearable Lightness of Being" by Milan Kundera. "In the Dream House" by Carmen Maria Machado. These outliers matter because they show I'm not in a complete filter bubble.

In Martech terms, these are the "discovery moments"‚Äîwhen the recommendation engine surfaces something unexpected that resonates. They're rare, but high-impact. They expand the universe of what's possible and prevent the echo chamber effect that happens with pure collaborative filtering.

What This Teaches Me About Data

Analyzing my own reading habits reinforces lessons I apply professionally:

  • Patterns Are Personal
    What looks like "average behavior" in aggregate masks deeply individual preferences. My fantasy obsession would be lost in averaged genre data.
  • Context Matters
    Reading velocity correlates with life events. Order frequency correlates with customer life stage. You can't interpret metrics without understanding context.
  • Loyalty Is Earned Through Quality
    I'll read anything Brandon Sanderson writes because he's never disappointed me. Same with brand loyalty‚Äîconsistency builds trust, trust builds retention.
  • Discovery Requires Risk
    My favorite outlier books came from recommendations that seemed "off-profile." Sometimes the algorithm needs to take calculated risks.
